# Hindi (hi) – Web Content Style Guide

Read `principles.md` first. Hindi-specific rules for SiteKit content (Devanagari script).

## Address & formality
- **आप (aap)** is the safe, respectful default for addressing readers on a public site. **तुम (tum)** is informal/friendly; **तू (tū)** is intimate and usually too casual for web content. Verbs and pronouns agree with the level you choose, so keep it consistent.
- A polite, clear register works for most sites; the `जी` honorific suffix adds respect where natural.

## Script & punctuation
- End full sentences with the **danda `।`** (पूर्ण विराम) in running Hindi prose; the Latin period `.` is common when text mixes Latin script, but `।` reads as properly Hindi.
- Question and exclamation marks `?` `!` are used as in English.

## Numbers & dates
- Western Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3) are standard online and in tech contexts (Devanagari digits १-९ exist but are less common on the web). Be consistent.
- Dates: `14 जून 2026` or `14/06/2026`.

## Capitalization & terminology
- Devanagari has **no upper/lower case**, so the title-case vs sentence-case question does not apply – just write naturally.
- Tech vocabulary is heavily borrowed from English; keep widely-understood loanwords (often transliterated, e.g. `ब्लॉग`, `न्यूज़लेटर`, `डाउनलोड`), and keep brand/product names in their original form. Don't over-Sanskritize – use the word readers actually use.

## Common pitfalls
- **Gender agreement:** nouns are masculine or feminine and verbs/adjectives inflect to match. When the reader's gender is unknown, prefer neutral phrasing, plural, or constructions that avoid forcing a gendered verb.
- Don't translate English word order literally – Hindi is subject-object-verb. Rewrite for natural flow.
