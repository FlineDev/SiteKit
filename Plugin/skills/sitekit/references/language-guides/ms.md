# Malay (ms) – Web Content Style Guide

Read `principles.md` first. Malay-specific rules for SiteKit content (Latin/Rumi script).

## Address & formality
- **"anda"** is the neutral, polite "you" for most public sites. **"awak"** / **"kamu"** are casual. For the first person, `saya` is the neutral "I"; `aku` is casual. In formal address, honorifics (`Encik`, `Puan`, `Cik`) precede names. Pick a register and keep it.

## Quotation marks
- Use English-style " … " (and ' … ' for nested).

## Numbers & dates
- Malaysia uses a **decimal point** and a **comma for thousands** (`1,234.50`), like English. Currency: `RM1,234.50` (symbol before, no space). Don't reformat numbers inside placeholders.
- Dates: `14 Jun 2026` or `14/06/2026`.

## Grammar & terminology
- No grammatical gender and no verb tense conjugation – time is shown by context or adverbs (`sudah`, `akan`, `sedang`), not verb endings. Don't invent tenses when translating from English.
- Plurals are formed by **reduplication** (`buku-buku`) or a quantifier (`banyak buku`), not an `-s`. Use reduplication only when number is not already clear.
- Tech vocabulary borrows heavily from English (`komputer`, `teknologi`, `pelayan` for server); keep widely-used loanwords and standard terms. Common web terms: `pos`/`artikel` (post), `langgan` (subscribe), kept `newsletter`/`surat berita`, `privasi` (privacy).

## Capitalization & common pitfalls
- **Sentence case** for headings. Month and day names **are** capitalized (`Januari`, `Isnin`); in `bahasa Melayu` the language word `bahasa` stays lowercase while `Melayu` is capitalized.
- Malay is more concise than English – cut filler rather than translating word for word.
